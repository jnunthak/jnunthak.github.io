CFD Modeling of the Glenn L. Martin Wind Tunnel

Advised by Dr. Jewel Barlow

Purpose

The Glenn L. Martin Wind tunnel is a classic subsonic wind tunnel actively involved in research and development since 1949. During initial commissioning time, it was determined to install vortex generators to alleviate separation in the first diffuser. In revisiting these designs, it was ultimately determined that simulation of the entire internal circuit is needed to capture the influences of significant features. A CFD model of the circuit needed to be developed to investigate flow uniformity and power requirements for a given test section speed.

Method

The CFD simulation software STAR-CCM+ was used to handle mesh generation and repair, solving, and post-processing. The project has been heavily reliant on the high-performance computing (HPC) resources at the University of Maryland. I wrote Shell scripts to submit jobs, Java macros to automate STAR-CCM+, and Python to document job statistics. I wrote a Python script that calculates inflation layer parameters based on turbulent boundary layer theory to target specific y+. Meshes of the 3,500-cubic-meter circuit on the order of 200 million to 800 million cells were created. The axial fan was modeled by a periodic pressure jump boundary condition with pressure jump values chosen to match physical operating conditions. To improve the simulation, various levels of mesh refinement, turbulence models, and dynamic boundary conditions were explored. Simulations are validated by comparing simulated and actual test section velocities.

Results

For a 70 MPH test section case, the CFD model test section velocity prediction was improved from over 20% to 6%. Computed data verify flow separation in the diffuser and recirculation zones downstream of the fan. Future work involves assessing simulation validity for other test cases and reducing the uncertainty of experimental measurements of the validation metrics, as well as applying adaptive mesh refinement. Extensive use of LaTeX for documentation. Currently drafting a manuscript and presentation for the AIAA Region III Student Conference.

CFD-based Surrogate Model for Predicting Aerodynamics of ROBIN Fuselages

Advised by Dr. James Baeder

Purpose

To determine if a machine learning surrogate model for helicopter fuselage geometries can be obtained from low-fidelty CFD data, and investigate its use in inverse design of helicopter fuselages.

Method

I wrote a Python script that generates an STL representation of ROBIN and ROBIN-MOD7 helicopter fuselages based on ROBIN super ellipse parameterization, and created CFD surface meshes using Autodesk Meshmixer. I interfaced my code with the OpenVSP Python API to automate native geometry generation and to execute the VSPAero panel method solver and to extract and plot the data.

Results

The study showed that panel method and Euler solvers do not have the sufficient fidelity for resolving helicopter fuselage geometry due to the bluff body shape and flow separation in the helicopter ramp. In-house RANS solvers are able to produce data that more closely matches experimental data for training a surrogate model. Current efforts are in improving surface mesh generation using GMSH and OpenFOAM cfmesh and exploring alternate 3D parameterization approaches such as piecewice spline.

Vertically Integrated Projects (VIPS): Go with the Flow! Empowering Hands-on Fluid Dynamics Education

Advised by Dr. Kenneth Kiger

Purpose

Research and develop a wind tunnel kit that can be used by students within their own home to supplement their coursework in fundamental fluid dynamics concepts through experiential learning. The kit is initially evaluated at $150.

Method

As a member of the boundary layer and fan characteristic subteam, I collaborated within a team of ~10 multidisciplinary engineering students to design a modular wind tunnel kit. I conducted a literature review to inform design of a boundary layer module and fan characteristic modules. Using SOLIDWORKS, I managed assemblies and helped create various 3D CAD parts including the iris mechanism shown on the left. The boundary layer and fan characteristic modules, meant for exploration of Bernoulli's principle, flow separation, and fan performance curves. I kept a personal notebook throughout the project to document my progress and my teammates' contributions. I also created renders and animations to showcase our designs with SOLIDWORKS. Recently, I assisted in validation of the latest prototype thrust stand experiment.

Results

Experimental validation has shown that the designed thrust stand performs efficiently and is axisymmetric, and thrust was measured with our manometer with a 0.3% accuracy. Measurements downstream of the nozzle shows an increase in error due to flow entrainment and turbulent mixing. I presented our work alongside 2 other students at APS DFD 2024 to other fluid mechanics educators. Currently drafting a manuscript for ASEE 2025.
